# Dreaming on demand

Generative AI is an exquisite technology. However, I can't help but feel that its true context is fundamentally misunderstood. Currently, enormous efforts are being put into trying to make it behave in ways that aren't in its nature. It seems to me like we're trying to teach a giraffe to pilot a helicopter, and I don't think it will work out.

Here's my suggestion. Generative AI is NOT a thinking machine and never was. It's a dream machine. It makes up spectacular dreams, and it's up to us whether or not we wish to engage with them, and how.

I feel this is an important distinction, so let me elaborate. Generative AI does not "really" hallucinate, it dreams. A hallucination is a state of affairs that differs from the established norm of operation. This is not the case for generative AI. Hallucination is its default mode. Dreaming is its default behavior. When AI hallucinates, that is what it's made to do. AI does not think, ruminate, or ponder. It does not reason. It simply dreams, and it's not unlike the dreams humans experience. We could even go so far as to say that just like humans, it draws from a sort of Jungian-style collective unconscious because it's been trained on enormous amounts of human data. If it were trained on Zeta-Reticulean data, then it would output Zeta-Reticulean dreams. But since it was trained on human data, the dreams it creates are human-like, and therefore feel familiar to us. These "hallucinations" are a slice of our collective unconscious, expressed concretely through a technology that allows us to sort of dream on demand.

So what, right? Why does this matter in practice?

Understanding AI as a dream machine isn't just for semantics. It fundamentally changes how we interact with it. I suggest that a tweak or two to our perspective may unlock a different, perhaps more optimal mental model for dealing with this technology in auspicious ways.

Looking at this image, you may notice an interesting phenomenon. If you just quickly glanced over the image above and didn't pay much attention to it, it looks "about right". In fact, it looks so "about right" that, were you in a hurry, you may just assume it is right and not give the matter further thought.

But as soon as you start actually paying attention to it and noticing all its various details, you quickly realize that it's full of inconsistencies and basically completely wrong from top to bottom. Unless you wanted your kids to believe X is for Zebra, I think you wouldn't want them learning the alphabet from this particular poster.

And here we run into the core issue of all types of generative AI. This exact same thing happens in all contexts where generative AI is used. The AI dreams up something that looks plausible and "about right". When it's used for creative writing, the output looks "about right". But when you actually take the time to read it, the writing seems flat, or off, or just plain inconsistent. When it's used for artwork, the same thing happens. The art looks "about right", at first glance anyway, but as soon as you start actually looking at it and paying attention to detail, you notice strange features like objects morphing into one another, inconsistent lighting or features that make no sense. But, as long as you don't pay attention to it, it looks "about right".

The same phenomenon happens when generative AI is used for coding. In fact, it's much more subtle and even sneakier. Code can look "about right", it can compile and when you try it out it even works, and maybe it even passes the tests. Everything looks right. So how on Earth could it possibly be bad?

Well, if you've ever seen a codebase that was "vibecoded" to any degree (I hate that term and I think it should be renamed to "unconscious coding") or that has any amount of unvetted AI code at all, you will be quick to notice that a lot of the code makes absolutely no sense. It's drowning in abstraction hell, there's dead code everywhere, the architectural choices are questionable, there's inefficient code and sometimes outright dangerous code. But it all seems written competently, there's comments everywhere providing confident signaling that this code is, apparently, A-OK. But the more you look at it the more you find parts that make no sense and it's often very hard to tell exactly what is going on. This is because AI code is not built on reasoning, it's built on dreams. It's very sneaky in the way it gets under your skin because you often find that it dreams up just the right thing you need, until you find yourself trusting it way too much for your own good. To build products on such code is clearly an exercise in building a house on sand.

Of course this is not universally the case and many teams do use AI successfully to push code. However, I would wager that the degree of their success depends heavily on the quality of their vetting process. And the reason why AI code requires so much vetting to begin with, is because the human entity is the only thing in the universe (that we know of) that can turn AI hallucinations into workable code.

Generative AI creates output that is plausible, but rarely accurate. This behavior is domain and scale independent. This is the core feature of generative AI and thus there's nothing to fix, it's already working optimally.

With generative AI, humanity has unlocked dreaming on demand. It's a cool technology. But maybe we shouldn't be treating it as a thinking machine (trying to substitute our own lucidity of thought in futile attempts to make the AI behave in ways it can't) and instead use it like we would any other type of dream, for inspiration. And then we can use for good.

# What generative AI is not

## An all-knowing oracle

Generative AI isn't a source of truth. It doesn't know anything the way a human expert or even a database does. It uses patterns from its training data to create plausible-sounding answers. It serves you confident answers which you cannot (without prior domain expertise) tell apart from complete falsehoods.

## A reliable narrator

The clearest example of the dream-like nature of generative AI is storytelling, because it can easily begin a tale beautifully, convincingly mimicking styles and generating prose that reads well. But push it further and dreaming takes over - characters change names or personalities mid-story, plotlines vanish, timelines contradict themselves, and details that mattered earlier are forgotten.

Like any dream, it feels coherent when we're in it, when we're not paying attention, when we're sort of unconscious. But when we "wake up" (start paying attention), the illusion becomes obvious.

This makes generative AI wonderful for brainstorming, world-building, or making first drafts - the sort of stuff that starts creative juices flowing. But it is an awful, terrible replacement for a human writer who can maintain internal consistency, intentionality and, heaven forbid, a hint of personality.

## An autonomous agent

Generative AI can't make decisions. Since it lacks intention, it cannot make meaningful choices and therefore will never be able to act autonomously. True autonomy requires a continuous feedback loop with reality.

If a human engineer writes a bug, the compiler throws an error, the engineer feels the friction, reasons through the failure, and fixes it.

An AI agent lacks this reality check. Because its default and only mode is dreaming, it cannot distinguish between successful execution and a plausible-looking failure. Left to its own devices, an autonomous agent will loop infinitely inside its own hallucinations, all the while confidently breaking things in the real world.

This is not a quirk of generative AI, this is its core feature.

# What AI is good at

Let's now examine what generative AI is actually good at. When we stop expecting a giraffe to pilot a helicopter, we can appreciate what it's actually good at.

## It excels at divergence

You can use AI as a shotgun approach to creativity. Humans naturally prefer linear, convergent thought - we find a good lane and we stick to it. AI is the opposite, it excels at divergence - taking a single concept and exploding it into fifty different variations. Most of these variations will be completely useless, but because there are so many of them, you can pick one and work from that point on. This is excellent for any sort of creative work, especially for defeating creative blockages.

## It's fantastic at style transfer and remixing

Because the dream machine has digested the sum total of human cultural output, it understands the *vibe* of specific eras, genres and creators. It can cross-pollinate concepts with ease. You can tell it to "Write a database migration script in the style of a 1940s noir detective novel" and it will gladly do so. It does not understand the underlying meaning of noir, but it's able to dream in the appropriate textures. This is absurdly good for idea iteration.

## You can be very, very specific with your prompts

Easily one of the best features of generative AI.

The reason why this is so good is because reality often hands a set of very specific requirements. If you ask a human designer for "a retro-futuristic toaster from the 1970s, but it's made of bioluminescent moss and can play NES games" they might ask you for a reality check. But the AI doesn't care, it takes your hyper-specific constraints and outputs something vivid. It bridges the gap between wild, abstract imagination and immediate visual or textual manifestation.

The fact that you can ask it anything whatsoever and it will output *something* (even if it's a complete fabrication) is a particularly useful property because it allows you to iterate through extremely specific ideas.

# How to actually use it well

If generative AI is a dream machine, then using it effectively requires us to become **lucid dreamers**. We must maintain our real-world waking awareness while navigating its subconscious landscape. Here's how we can accomplish that in practice.

## For inspiration

This is where the dream machine truly shines. It can reverse-engineer your own creative vision.

Instead of asking the AI to build the final product, use it to generate high-fidelity references of what was already in your mind. Then study those references. Then make more references. Then study those.

Look at these references not as finished products, but as a map of possibilities.

## For expanding searches

When you're researching any topic, you typically run into more and more nuance and specifics, until you sort of hit a wall and you have to find alternative sources to keep going. Generative AI bypasses this process and allows you to expand your searches indefinitely, in any direction you choose to go.

## For first drafts, mockups or reference

Use it to build the scaffolding, but not the house. It's brilliant at creating all sorts of standard boilerplates for almost any creative task you can think of. It's also extremely good for reference art of any sort.

## As an interactive rubber duck (easily the best use case)

Software engineers have long used "rubber duck debugging" - explaining their code problems to a plastic duck until they spot their own mistake. Practice shows that just by describing your problem well, the solution often appears. In this context, generative AI is like the ultimate rubber duck because it actually talks back to you.

## Treat its output as a starting point, not the end result

The moment you copy-paste AI output directly into a production codebase without intense scrutiny, you have fallen asleep at the wheel. You are letting the machine dream for you. The output of an AI should always be treated as raw material that requires further processing which can only be done by an actual human.

## Take breaks from it

Because the dream machine provides instant gratification and endless novelty, it has addictive qualities. If you rely on it continuously, your own creative muscles will begin to atrophy. Your own thinking may become lazy and flat. To combat this, take breaks from it. You will find that you start using AI much more creatively when you deliberately take breaks from it and confirm (to yourself) that you don't actually rely on it.

# Conclusions

Generative AI is here to stay and the best we can do is learn how to use it well. For this reason I believe it's in our best interest to stop treating it as a thinking machine, because it's simply not one. While it can convincingly mimic thought processes, it does not, in fact, possess them.

Instead, we should treat generative AI as a system not for substituting, but for augmenting creativity through controlled dreaming.

As always, use it wisely.
